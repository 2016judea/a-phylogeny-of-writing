Literature, read by machine.

A decade of turning books into data — using NLP and network science to study prose, voice, and the shape of genre itself.

For most of its history, literature has been classified by hand — the canon, the genres, the lineages of influence all drawn by critics working from opinion and memory. I've spent years asking a different question: what does fiction look like when you treat it as data?

The work below runs from early NLP experiments on prose and voice, done on my own before graduate school, into the primary research I carried out at Columbia University — modeling the whole landscape of fiction as a network to watch its genres form and mutate over time.

Literature Mutations ↗

Genres are literature's main system of classification — yet the rate at which they form and mutate has only ever been judged qualitatively, by experts working on opinion. This research asks whether that rate can be measured.

It models fiction as a graph G = (V, E): each novel is a node, and an edge is drawn between two books whose genre profiles — built from the weighted distribution of their Goodreads shelves — are similar enough to clear a threshold function. Grown year by year along publication dates and run through graph-clustering algorithms, genres and subgenres emerge from the data itself rather than from a critic's hand — making it possible to study how fast new genres appear and forecast genre mutation forward.
